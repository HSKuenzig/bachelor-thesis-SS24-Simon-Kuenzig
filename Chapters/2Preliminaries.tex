In order to understand how an application can be designed to \enquote{learn} or extract and process knowledge from any textbook such \citet{bb2}, we must first deconstruct the underlying technologies and processes.
To illustrate the approach presented, let us consider the example of a student preparing for an exam in \enquote{Medical Informatics}.
That means that the student needs relevant knowledge.
Therefore, the student wants to extract this specific, in relation to specific questions, knowledge from a book, such as \citet{bb2}.
Instead of reading the entire book each time, the student could use the index to look up relevant knowledge within specific chapters (\textit{retrieval}) or try to memorise the entire book content (\textit{large context}).
This chapter explains the technology that enables computers to perform precisely these different methods of knowledge acquisition: From \textbf{reading} the text (\ac{nlp}) to \textbf{understanding} and \textbf{reasoning} with it (\textit{Transformer}).
From \textbf{finding} and \textbf{extracting} the respective knowledge to \textbf{answering} the students questions (\ac{qa}).
All this based on the entire content (= \ac{lcw}) of \citetitle{bb2}  or only certain parts of it (= \ac{rag}).

\section{Knowledge Base}\label{sec:knowledgeb}
As explained in the introduction, \citet{bb2} serves as the knowledge base for answering questions using \acp{lm}.
It is therefore essential to first understand why this book serves this purpose.\\
\enquote{In order to professionally deal with \ac{his} [\dots] well-educated informaticians are needed}\citep[p.e40]{Ammenwerth2017FvS}.
Therefore, leading experts in the field of medical informatics, including Winter, Ammenwerth and Haux, have already published the third edition of this book.
The book teaches the essential scientific foundations for the field of medical informatics. 
It defines the precise terms and theoretical frameworks necessary for analysing, understanding and potentially improving complex socio-technical systems within health care.
Among the most important contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Information and knowledge logistics:}
    In \citet{bb2} this is defined as the goal of \ac{his} \enquote{aiming at making the right information and knowledge available at the right time, at the right place, to the right people, and in the right form so that these people can make the right decisions.}\citep[p.19]{bb2}
    This heuristic serves as an effective evaluation metric to assess the quality of information systems within a \ac{his}.
    %
    \item \textbf{Interoperability standards:} A comprehensive overview of modern healthcare standards is provided and explained in context.
    These syntactic standards (e.g, \textit{HL7 V2}) and semantic standards (e.g., \textit{SNOMED}) are the essential and necessary basis of modern institutional and transinstitutional \textit{health information systems}.
    \item \textbf{The $\text{3LGM}^2$ metamodel:} A three-layer architecture framework (\textit{domain}, \textit{logic} and \textit{physical} layer), introduced in \citet{Winter2003threelgm2}, that enables precise documentation, description and management of a respective \ac{his}.
    The book describes and explains the model in detail and makes it accessible through direct examples and exercises.
\end{itemize}
\subsection{From Hospital to Health}
\citet{bb2} is a seminal work in the field and documents the profound changes in \textit{medical informatics} over the last two decades.
The book emphasises and identifies the necessary transition from an institution-orientated focus and perspective towards person-centred care models.
This transition represents the modern understanding that health is a continuous state that is influenced across different \textit{life situations}.
Within the scenarios of \enquote{life situations} information processing expands from self-contained \enquote{Hospital Information Systems} to \enquote{Health Information Systems} that are transinstitutionally interconnected.
Thereby, including institutions, such as rehabilitation centers, nursing homes, outpatient clinics and the patient's personal environment. 
The resulting comprehensive, interconnected health information systems require efficient and high-quality \textbf{information and knowledge logistics}.

\enquote{\textit{Information and knowledge logistics} aims at making the right information and knowledge available at the right time, at the right place, to the right people,
and in the right form so that these people can make the right decisions.}\citep[p.19]{bb2}

\section{Natural Language Processing (NLP)}\label{sec:nlp_prel}
The fundamental goal of this work is to have a computer system understand and answer questions based on text from \citet{bb2}.
\begin{definition}
    \ac{nlp} \enquote{is concerned with the automated processing of human language. It addresses the analysis and generation of written and spoken language [\dots].
    [\dots] \ac{nlp} can be seen as the applied side of computational linguistics [\dots] concerned with formal analysis and computational modeling of language at the intersection of linguistics, computer science, and psychology.}\citep[p.1]{meurer2021nlp}
\end{definition}
%\citet{meurer2021nlp}
The importance of \ac{nlp} for current research and application has increased significantly, particularly with the development of powerful \acp{lm} that can accomplish complex linguistic tasks.
\ac{nlp} focuses on tasks such as question answering, summarisation, named entity recognition, and machine translation, all of which require a nuanced understanding of both syntax and semantics.
This work focusses on two specific sub-disciplines of \ac{nlp}:
\textit{Information Retrieval} and \textit{Question Answering}.
%
\subsection{Information Retrieval}\label{subsec:ir}
Before a system can answer a question about a specific topic, it must retrieve the relevant information and knowledge.
%
\begin{definition}\label{def:ir}
\enquote{Information retrieval (IR) is finding material (usually documents) of
an unstructured nature (usually text) that satisfies an information need
from within large collections (usually stored on computers).}\citep[p.1]{ManningRaghavanSchuetze2008ir}
\end{definition}
%
In the context of this thesis \ac{ir} describes the mechanism used to find the specific pages or paragraphs within \citet{bb2} that contain the answer to a question of a student or practitioner of \textit{medical informatics}.
Traditionally, this involved keyword matching (e.g., searching for \enquote{hospital}, \enquote{information}, \enquote{system}).
Modern \ac{ir} enables \textit{Semantic Search}, where the system retrieves information based on meaning and context (see \cref{sec:embeddings}, \cref{sec:vector_simil_search}) rather than simply matching words.
This retrieval process is the \enquote{R} in one in \ac{rag} (\textbf{R}etrieval \textbf{A}ugmented \textbf{G}eneration) of the methods investigated, further described in \cref{sec:rag_prel}.
%
\subsection{Question Answering}\label{subsec:question_answering_prel}
%
Once the information and knowledge are retrieved and accessible, the system is supposed to generate an answer.
\begin{definition}
\ac{qa} is an area within \ac{nlp} and \ac{ir} that focusses on the development of systems designed to answer questions from users in natural language. 
The entered question is first analysed to extract the intention of the query. 
Based on this, relevant information is retrieved from a source, further processed using \ac{ir} technologies and then combined to a coherent answer \citep{Manning2008}.
\end{definition}
%
\ac{qa} can be categorised by their domain and answer type:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Open-Domain vs. Closed-Domain:} \textbf{Open} \ac{qa} answers questions using general knowledge (e.g., \enquote{What is a computer?}). 
    \textbf{Closed}-domain \ac{qa} is restricted to a specific source or subject, such as the \citet{bb2} scientific textbook.
    \item \textbf{Extractive vs. Generative} \textbf{Extractive} \ac{qa} highlights the exact location of the respective text in the source.
    During \textbf{Generative} \ac{qa}, an \ac{lm} generates a natural language answer based on the source text/knowledge base.
\end{itemize}
%
\section{Language Models}\label{sec:lm}
\ac{lm} are the main topic in current research to solve \ac{nlp} tasks.
%
\begin{definition}\label{def:lm}
    \textbf{Language Models} are advanced machine learning models.
    They are designed to process, understand and generate human language.
\end{definition}
In order to understand and generate human language, \acp{lm} are trained on various data using machine learning algorithms. 
They have a wide range of applications, including translation, text generation, speech recognition, and text classification.
However, to process language in text form, \ac{lm} do not simply \enquote{read} raw text like a student or practitioner.
The input must be prepared and transformed into a format that the model can compute.
The main part of that transformational process is the segmentation of text into discrete units (\textit{tokens}).
These \textit{tokens} are then mapping into a numerical vector space (\textit{embeddings})
%
\subsection{Tokens and Tokenisation}\label{subsec:token_prel}
%
\begin{definition}\label{def:token}
    In \ac{nlp}, a \textit{token} is a distinct linguistically meaningful substructure of a text.
    It is used as input to \ac{lm}.
\end{definition}
%
Tokens can represent multiple and different linguistic units, including:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Full words} (e.g., \textit{hospital})
  \item \textbf{Subwords:} (e.g., \texttt{inter} + \texttt{oper} + \texttt{ability} (interoperability)
  \item \textbf{Individual characters}, especially in character-level models (e.g., \textit{a,e,i,o,!?})
\end{itemize}
%
The individual approach on how to segment them, directly affects the quality and interpretability of the model output.
This is mainly due to the way \acp{lm} process sequences of numerical vectors derived from these \textit{tokens}.
\textit{Tokens}, therefore, form the basic building blocks of \ac{lm} and are indispensable for processing structured data.
%
\subsubsection{The Necessity of Tokenisation}
%
\begin{definition}
    \textit{Tokenisation} is the process of converting the initial input text into \textit{tokens}.
\end{definition}
%
\acp{lm} process text in the form of \textbf{tokens}. 
The efficient and structured conversion of potentially huge text input into \textit{token} via the process of \textit{tokenisation} is therefore essential to control memory and computational requirements.
%
The division of the raw text into \textit{tokens} is done according to a specific tokenisation algorithm.
For example, in the case of the \ac{bpe}-algorithm, frequent character sequences are iteratively merged to form subword \textit{tokens}.
This enables efficient handling of words that are not in the vocabulary (out-of-vocabulary \textit{OOV}).
This is important as unfamiliar words can be decomposed into known subwords or, if necessary, replaced by special unknown \textit{tokens}, while preserving semantic structure.

The following shows the tokenisation process using an example question from the question set:
%
\begin{quote}
    \textbf{Question}: \textit{Define the term \enquote{hospital information system}.}
\end{quote}
The standard tokeniser processes as a sequence of distinct units, \textit{tokens}.
The particular segmentation of the term \enquote{hospital information system} into full words or subwords depends on the predefined vocabulary and the \textit{tokenisation algorithm} (see \cref{subsub:token_algorithm}).

\subsubsection{Tokenisation Algorithms}\label{subsub:token_algorithm}
Modern \ac{lm} rely on statistics-based, data-driven tokenisation algorithms to efficiently process extensive and diverse vocabularies.
The most common algorithms are \textit{Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)} and \textit{WordPiece}.
%
\subsubsection{Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)}
\begin{definition}
    \textbf{Byte-Pair Encoding} (BPE) is a frequency-based compression algorithm adapted for \ac{nlp} by \citet{sennrich2016neural}. 
\end{definition}
%
The \textit{BPE} algorithm starts by transforming the input text into a character- and byte-level representation.
Then, the most frequent pairs of subsequent characters or bytes are iteratively merged into new \textit{tokens}.
This transformative process continues until a predefined vocabulary size is reached.
The result is a compressed representation of the original text.
The overall goal is the ideal balance of vocabulary compactness and semantic expressiveness.\\
%
The \ac{bpe} algorithm follows a deterministic process and is easily scalable.
This makes it widely adopted in \textit{Transformer}-based models.

\subsubsection{WordPiece}
\begin{definition}
    \textbf{WordPiece} is a subword tokenisation algorithm developed by \textit{Google}.
    The algorithm determines necessary merges based on likelihood maximisation rather than frequency. It was first popularised through its use in \ac{bert} \citep{devlin2019bert} and has since been widely adapted.
\end{definition}
\textit{WordPiece} was originally developed for machine translation tasks at \textit{Google}.
Similarly to \ac{bpe}, \textit{WordPiece} creates subword units of the original terms.
At the same time, \textit{WordPiece} selects merge operations based on likelihood maximisation rather than simple frequency.
Overall, the algorithm improves both model generalisation and semantic understanding.
%
\subsection[Consistent Tokenisation]
Consistent \textit{tokenisation} is essential to ensure that the input is standardised for the \ac{lm}.
This enables the \ac{lm} to process the input reliably and in a reproducibly manner.
Since Transformer-based \ac{lm} rely on token-level embeddings, inconsistencies in the segmentation from text to \textit{tokens} can lead to significantly different results.
Even if the underlying input text remains unchanged.
%
This is particularly relevant in relation to the following points:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reproducible benchmarking, as pursued in this thesis
    \item objective model comparison, when evaluating multiple \ac{lm} or configurations
    \item Resource efficiency, since longer \textit{token} sequences incur greater memory and computational costs
\end{itemize}
%
In practice, a text input that results in $120$ \textit{tokens} for one model could be split into $180$ \textit{tokens} by another model based on differences in tokenisation (see examples at \cref{subsub:example_token}.
This variation affects latency, computational cost, and model comprehension, especially in length-sensitive architectures.
%
\subsubsection[Vocabulary and Mapping]{Predefined Vocabulary and Token-to-ID Mapping}
%
\begin{definition}
    A \textbf{predefined vocabulary} is a finite list of textual \textit{tokens}.
    This finite list is established during the construction of the \textit{tokeniser}, which is typically done before the training phase.
    The vocabulary can, for example, include full words, frequent subwords or domain-specific terminology.
\end{definition}
%
When using static tokenisation algorithms such as \textit{BPE} or \textit{Wordpiece}, this vocabulary remains unchanged during the application phase of the model.
This ensures consistency between training and subsequent application.
%
The structure and composition of the vocabulary depends on the applied \textit{tokenisation} algorithm.
For example, \textit{tokens} such as \enquote{inter}, \enquote{oper}, and \enquote{ability} may represent common subword components, thus resulting in a separation of the original term.
At the same time, \enquote{hospital} and \enquote{systems} could be persisted as individual \textit{tokens}.
%
Vocabulary sizes typically range between $10,000$ and $50,000$ individual entries.
The overall goal is the ideal balance between coverage and computational efficiency.
%
%
\subsection{Encoding}\label{sec:encoding}
%
\begin{definition}
    \textbf{Encoding} describes the conversion of a \textit{token} into its numerical identifier \textit{token ID}.
\end{definition}
%
Each \textit{token ID} is used as an index in the model embedding matrix.
This matrix stores the corresponding high-dimensional vector representations of tokens.
Therefore, during the encoding process this finite list of (\textit{vocabulary}) entries serves as a search table for mapping between textual units and numerical representation.
This mapping is foundational for \ac{lm} such as those based on the \textit{transformer} architectures.
It enables the \ac{lm} to transform human language, presented to the \ac{lm} in text format, into a mathematically machine-processable format.\\

\section{Embeddings}\label{sec:embeddings}
%
For \acp{lm}, these \textit{embeddings} function as the transformative bridge between symbolic representations of language and the numerical input required by the underlying machine learning architecture of \ac{lm}.
These vector-based representations encode both semantic and syntactic information.
Their purpose is to capture the relationships between words and contextual nuances in language in machine-processable format.
In the context of \acp{lm}, \textit{transformer}-based architectures and \ac{rag}, embeddings are fundamental for the understanding of meaning, managing context, and enabling context-awareness even over long sequences.
%
\subsection{Semantic Embeddings}
%
\begin{definition}\label{def:semantic_embed}
\textbf{Semantic embeddings} are dense vector representations of individual \textit{tokens} (e.g., words, subwords).
They encode syntactic and semantic relationships based on distributional properties of language~\citep{Mikolov2013EfficientEO, pennington2014glove}.
\end{definition}
%
\subsubsection{Static Semantic Embeddings}
%
In earlier neural models, such as \textit{Word2Vec}~\citep{Mikolov2013EfficientEO} and \textit{GloVe}~\citep{pennington2014glove}, \textit{embeddings} were \enquote{learned} by predicting how often words appear together.
These static \textit{embeddings} assign a single vector to each word.
This mapping is independent of linguistic context, which limits their ability to disambiguate polysemous terms (e.g., \enquote{bank} as a financial institution versus a \enquote{riverbank}).
%
\subsubsection{Contextual Semantic Embeddings}
%
\textit{Transformer}-based models try to overcome this limitation with by using \textit{contextual embeddings}. 
In \textit{contextual embeddings} a \textit{token} \textit{embedding}, in form of a vector, dynamically reflects its meaning based on the surrounding text.
These contextualised vectors are generated dynamically as the model processes input sequences through \textit{self-attention} layers.
For example, the \textit{token} \enquote{hospital} can lead to different embeddings when used in the context of \enquote{hospital information system} compared to \enquote{hospital management}.
%
\subsection{Positional Embeddings}\label{sub:position_embed_prel}
%
Unlike recurrent or convolutional models, \textit{Transformer} architectures lack an inherent mechanism to capture the sequential order of \textit{tokens}. 
Attention mechanisms operate in parallel, all input \textit{tokens} are processed simultaneously, making it impossible for \ac{lm} to recognise word order unless it is explicitly encoded.
%
\subsubsection{Necessity of Positional Information}
Therefore, \textit{Transformers} require an additional mechanism to inject sequence information into the input of the respective \ac{lm}.
This is achieved by using \textit{positional embeddings}.
%
\begin{definition}
\textbf{Positional embeddings} are vectors that are combined with the individual \textit{token} embeddings.
This combination ecnodes the position of a \textit{token} in a sequence.
The positional parameter enables an order-aware processing in models based on self-attention~\citep{vaswani2017attention}.
\end{definition}
%
\subsubsection{Types of Positional Embeddings}

There are two primary types of \textit{positional embeddings}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Absolute positional embeddings}:
    Each position in the sequence is assigned a unique fixed vector.
    This vector is often derived from sinusoidal functions as introduced by \citet{vaswani2017attention}.
    \item \textbf{Relative positional embeddings}:
    Relative positional embeddings encode the relative distance between \textit{tokens}, rather than their absolute positions. 
    This approach has been shown to improve generalisation across sequence lengths~\citep{shaw2018self, dai2019transformer}.
\end{itemize}
%
Relative positional representations allow \acp{lm} to focus on inter-token relations and dependencies without being limited by fixed input lengths.
This enhances the capabilities of traditional \textit{Transformers} by generalising to longer or variable-length inputs.
Such enhanced \textit{Transformers} are particularly useful in \ac{lm} designed for long-context retrieval.
%
\paragraph{Relevance of Tokenisation}
Understanding \textit{tokenisation} and its influence on the capabilities and performance of \ac{lm} is essential for \cref{task:1_1_llm_comparison}.
Different \acp{lm} allow for different context window (see \cref{sec:context_windows}) sizes as input based on the number of tokens.
Thus, \textit{token} and \textit{tokenisation} define and limit the possibilities of input in a potential application such as \ac{qa}, which is further highlighted in \cref{sec:large_context}.
%
\section{Language Model Architecture}\label{sec:lm_architecture}

Once the input text has been processed and converted into machine-readable representation (\textit{tokens} and \textit{embeddings}), the next relevant step is to process this data in order to extract and obtain its meaning.
%
This requires a system that is capable of processing sequential data, understanding complex dependencies and finally generating output, such as text in natural language.
In modern \ac{nlp} which is currently driven by \ac{lm}, this task is performed and enabled by \textit{neural networks}, specifically those built upon the \textit{Transformer} architecture. 
%
The \textit{Transformer} architecture has revolutionised \ac{nlp} by enabling \ac{lm} to process data sequences simultaneously (in parallel) rather than sequentially (one word or \textit{token}after the other).
This allows the system to capture complex dependencies and relationships within the text efficiently and cost-effectively and as the culmination of decades of machine learning progress, it is the foundation of modern \ac{lm}.
The basic architecture of the \textit{Transformer} has since been modified for individual applications.
%
\subsection{Neural Networks}\label{subsec:nn_prel}

To understand the architecture of the \textit{Transformer}, it is necessary to know the basics of neural networks.
\ac{nn} were first described by \citet{neuronal_networks_first} and have undergone many changes and improvements since then.
\begin{definition}
    \textbf{Neural Networks} are computational models inspired by the biological structure of the human brain and consist of interconnected layers of nodes (neurons) that process information through weighted connections~\citep{mcculloch1943logical}.
\end{definition}
%
\ac{nn} use mathematical equations to  model the functions of the human brain.
A \ac{nn} consists of a series of neurons that have input and output connections to other neurons.
If an individual threshold value is exceeded by the input signals, the respective triggered neuron then sends a signal as output.

In contrast to the brain, however, normal \acp{nn} have a more strictly logical structure.
The neurons are organised into layers.
The first layer is the \textit{input layer} with a certain number of input neurons.
The last layer is the \textit{output layer} with the output neurons.
The layers in-between are called \textit{hidden layers} and also consist of neurons.
A standard \ac{nn} has a \textit{input layer}, one or more \textit{hidden layers} and an \textit{output layer}.
Each neuron applies a non-linear activation function to the weighted sum of its inputs. This process enables the network to learn complex patterns and relationships within the processed data.
%
\subsubsection{Deep Learning}

\textit{Deep learning} extends, improves and builds upon the concept of \ac{nn}.
In \citet{hinton2006fast} it was demonstrated that \textit{deep} \ac{nn} could effectively learn complex data representations by stacking multiple hidden layers creating a \textit{deep hierarchical structure}.
In \textit{deep} \ac{nn}, the lower layers identify simple patterns.
The subsequent higher layers combine these patterns to represent increasingly abstract and complex concepts~\citep[p.~436]{lecun2015deep}.

\paragraph{Significance for modern \ac{nlp}}
%

The depth of these \acp{nn} allows for the modelling of complex non-linear relationships in natural language.
As a result \acp{nn} can \enquote{learn} high-level representations from raw text.
Traditional machine learning methods relied mainly on manual feature engineering.
\textit{Deep learning} marked the transition from such manual feature learning to automatic hierarchical learning.
This allows these networks to capture semantic nuances, ambiguities.
Furthermore, they \enquote{understand} sophisticated, complex syntactic structures within extensive information input (large context sizes).
This makes \acp{nn}, particularly in \acp{lm}, essential for tasks such as \ac{qa} based on compelex and comprehensive knowledge basis (e.g., \citetitle{bb2}).
%
\paragraph{Multimodality}
%
The inherent adaptability of \ac{nn} and the application of the \textit{deep learning} method has significantly expanded the applicability of machine learning.
By overcoming the limitations of single-modality (text), these architectures have enabled \textit{multimodal} capabilities.
This allows current \ac{sota} \ac{ai} systems to process and generate images, audio and videos.
Thus, significantly extending the possibilities of data and information processing.
%
\subsection{Transformer}\label{subsec:transformer}
%
\begin{definition}\label{def:transformer}
    A \textbf{Transformer} is a \ac{nn} architecture that was first introduced in \citet{vaswani2017attention}.
    The \textbf{Transformer} architecture uses \textit{attention mechanisms} to understand the relationships and dependencies of text units within sequences of text.
\end{definition}
%
As introduced by \citet{vaswani2017attention}, this specific neural network design, the \textit{Transfomer} architecture, established a paradigm shift in \ac{nlp}.
\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/transformer.png}
    \caption{The Transformer-Model Architecture \citep{vaswani2017attention}}
    \label{figure:transformer}
\end{figure}
It was developed primarily to address the fundamental limitations of the \textbf{Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq)} Encoder-Decoder framework introduced by \citet{sutskever2014sequence}.
In these recurrent \textit{Seq2Seq} models, the entire semantics of an input sequence must be compressed into a single, fixed-length context vector.
This creates a \enquote{bottleneck problem}, where the model struggles to preserve information from long complex sentences.
%
\paragraph{Self-Attention}
This architectural evolution of \ac{lm} by the introduction of the \textit{Transformer} is driven by the \textbf{self-attention mechanism}.
Instead of relying on a single context vector, \textit{attention} allows the model to \enquote{scan} the entire input sequence based on positional embedding (see \cref{sub:position_embed_prel}.
The mechanism then weighs the significance of different parts relative to each other, capturing widespread, global contextual dependencies. 
By calculating these relevance \textit{attention} weights, the model creates a direct mathematical shortcut to \enquote{look back} to distant context.
The model then focusses on the most relevant positions (source words) for the current generation step.
For example, it allows the model to understand that \enquote{\textbf{system}} is semantically related to \enquote{\textbf{hospital}} and \enquote{\textbf{information}}, regardless of their distance within the compound concept of a \enquote{\textbf{hospital information system}}.
%
Within the \textit{Transformer} architecture, this \textit{Self-Attention} mechanism is applied for both, understanding of the input and generating the output.
By overcoming the information bottleneck that causes the loss of important contextual relationships and meaning, the \textit{Transformer} captures widespread, global contextual dependencies with great effectiveness.
%
\subsubsection[Encoder-Decoder]{Encoder and Decoder in Transformer Models}\label{subsub:encoder_decoder_prel}
The original \textit{transformer} architecture uses these mechanisms within a structure composed of two primary components: the \textbf{encoder} model and the \textbf{decoder} model.
These components build a bidirectional architecture to process input sequences and generate appropriate outputs.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Encoder:} A stack of identical layers that process the input sequence in parallel.
    Each layer applies \textit{self-attention} mechanisms and \textit{feed-forward} networks to build contextualised representations of the input.
    The \textit{encoder} captures structural and semantic relationships between processed words (\textit{tokens}, see \cref{def:token}) and outputs them as compact representation in the form of a context vector.
    The parallel processing allows the model to build contextualised representations simultaneously, capturing complex structural and semantic relationships bi-directionally.
    
    This makes the \textit{encoder} ideal for tasks that require deep linguistic \enquote{understanding}.
    %
    \item \textbf{Decoder}: 
    The \textit{decoder} also consists of a stack of layers.
    The \textit{decoder} architecture implements a \textit{masked self-attention} and \textit{cross-attention} mechanism, in addition to the self-attention and feed-forward sublayers.
    
    The \textit{Masked self-attention} ensures that the prediction of a particular \textit{token} depends on the previously generated output tokens.
    By the implementation of \textit{masking}, the architecture maintains the \textit{autoregressive} property required for sequential generation.
    The \textit{cross-attention} mechanism allows the decoder to attend to the representations generated by the \textit{encoder}. 
    By integrating the information derived from the \textit{attention} mechanism the \textit{decoder} can focus on relevant parts of the input sequence to predict a target sequence and generate the target output effectively.
\end{itemize}
%
\paragraph{Pre-training Corpus}
The resulting advantages in scalability and performance have allowed for the training of models on massive pre-training corpora.
%
One such corpus is, for example, the \enquote{\textit{REFINEDWEB}, a high-quality five trillion tokens web-only English pretraining dataset}\citep{penedo2023refinedwebdatasetfalconllm}.
This corresponds to approximately 42 million times the content of \citet{bb2} and is just one of the corpora used today for the pre-training of \ac{llm}.\\
%
The foundational \textit{Transformer} model was particularly idealised for \textit{sequence-to-sequence} tasks, such as translating languages~\citep{vaswani2017attention}.
However, many later models have adapted and repurposed it for more specific applications. 
The basic structure has been continuously modified over time, forming the foundation for many modern \acp{lm} such as \textit{BERT} (only \textit{Encoder}), \textit{GPT} (only \textit{Decoder}), and \textit{T5} (\textit{Encoder-Decoder}).

These developments resulted in fundamental differences in the models and their ability to address a variety of \ac{nlp} problems~\citep{raffel2020exploring}.
%
The original transformer architecture can be seen at \cref{figure:transformer}.
\subsection{Functionality of Transformer Models}
%
The fundamental processing of a \textit{transformer} model can be separated into two phases:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{training} phase
    \item \textbf{inference} phase
\end{itemize}
%
During \textbf{training}, the internal model parameters are adjusted and optimised to minimise the prediction error.
\begin{definition}\label{def:learnable_par}
\textbf{Learnable parameters} are the internal variables within the \ac{lm} architecture that are adjusted during \textit{training} to minimise the prediction error metric.
\end{definition}
%
\begin{definition}\label{def:training}
    The \textbf{Training} phase is the fundamental learning period.
    During \textit{Training} a model iteratively adjusts its internal \textit{learnable parameters} based on provided dataset.
\end{definition}
The \textit{dataset} used for \textit{Training} is referred to as the \textit{training corpus}.
The primary goal of training a \ac{lm} is to minimise the prediction error metric.
This error metric is assessed by comparing the outputs generated by the model with the ground truth within the training data.
Thereby improving the ability of the model to generalise to new, unseen data.
\paragraph{Continual Pre-training}
General pre-training provides \acp{lm} with broad linguistic knowledge as the used training corpora used are usually immense.
In specialised domains, such as \textit{medical informatics}, even deeper expertise beyond these corpora is required. 
\begin{definition}\label{def:continual_pretaining}
At this point, \textbf{continual pre-training} describes the process of further training a previously pre-trained \ac{lm} on a new, specialised dataset.
In order to become individual experts in a domain or a specific task area, \ac{lm} are particularly \textbf{trained} for this purpose.
By aplying \textbf{Continual pre-training}, the respective model is adapted to a specific area.
\end{definition}
In this process, the weights of the respective model (see \cref{def:learnable_par}) are updated using new data while attempting to retain the knowledge from the initial training phase.
An example of this approach can be found in \citet{Paul_Keller}.
In the work presented a pre-trained \ac{lm} (\textit{Llama 2} model) was adapted to the medical knowledge contained in the textbook \citet{bb2} using \textit{continual pre-training}.
However, the approach resulted in unsatisfactory results (see \cref{ch:relatedWork}).
%
\subsubsection{Inference}\label{subsub:inference_prel}
The \textit{training} phase is followed by the \textit{inference} phase.
Both phases can be alternated during the development process of the respective \ac{lm}.
\begin{definition}\label{def:inference}
\textbf{Inference} describes the application phase of a finalised trained model to process a new input and compute output based on previously \textit{learned parameters}.
\end{definition}
%
At the point of \textit{inference}, a textual input sequence is initially \textit{tokenised} (\cref{subsec:token_prel} and \textit{embedded} (\cref{sec:embeddings}).
The \textit{embedded} representations of the initial sequence are then processed through the network layers using the architecture described in \cref{subsub:encoder_decoder_prel}.
In this dynamic autoregressive generation process, the model predicts one \textit{token} at a time.
Each generated \textit{token} is appended to the cumulative input sequence.
In the context of this work, the input consists of the entered prompt, as well as all tokens previously generated by the model in the current session.
The prompt incorporates the respective question, for example, posed by a student or practitioner, as well as additional knowledge, such as \citet{bb2}.
This additional knowledge, provided at inference time, is referred to as \textit{in-context learning} and is central to this thesis.
%
\section{The Challenge of Context Processing}\label{sec:context_windows}
The resulting growing sequence provides an extended context for subsequent predictions.
This leads to a critical dependency on the memory of the underlying system of the processing \ac{lm}.
The respective model must be able to hold this entire sequence in its active memory in order to maintain linguistic and factual coherence.
At this point, the relevance of the \textit{context window} becomes clear.
%
\subsection{Context Windows}
%
When the combined length of the input context and iteratively generated response exceed this limit, the model effectively loses its ability to \enquote{see} the earlier parts of the context within the current session.
This results in the loss of context, an interrupted chain of reasoning and potentially factual hallucinations.
%
\begin{definition}
A \textbf{context window} refers to the maximum number of input \textit{tokens} a \textit{Transformer} model can attend to at any point in time.
In autoregressive models, this includes both the input prompt and all \textit{tokens} generated during \textit{inference}.
\end{definition}
%
\subsubsection[Limitations and Advancements]{Limitations and Advancements in Large Context Processing}\label{subsec:limit_context_prel}
%
The scalability of context windows is fundamentally constrained by the architecture of the \textit{Transformer}.
The \textit{self-attention mechanism} compares every \textit{token} to every other token, resulting in quadratic computational complexity ($O(N^2)$).
As the sequence length $N$ increases, the required computations and memory grow exponentially.
This limited earlier models, such as
the \textit{BERT-base} model to only support $512$ \textit{tokens}~\citep{devlin2019bert}, which roughly corresponds to the first subsection \textit{$1.1$ \enquote{Motivation and Objective of the Book}} of the \enquote{Introduction} section in \citet{bb2}.
%
The size of the context windows has been continuously extended through iterations of development.
\textit{OpenAI}'s \textit{GPT-3}, commonly acknowledged as the start of \enquote{large} models ~\citep[p.1]{langlais2025commoncorpuslargestcollection}, is limited to $2048$ \textit{tokens}~\citep{gpt3} during a single \textit{inference} pass, allowing for an input slightly larger than the first two subsections of the introduction.
Recent architectural innovations have significantly expanded these limits, enabling \textbf{Large Context Windows (\textit{LCW}}) capable of directly processing a complete textbook.
However, there is no uniform definition or threshold for \enquote{large context windows}. 
Current models, such as \textit{GPT-$5$} and \textit{Anthropics} \textit{Claude $4$} can ingest up to a $1~million$ \textit{tokens}~\citep{openai2025gpt4, claude2025feature} allowing for significantly larger \textit{context windows}, an estimated $10$ times the book \citetitle{bb2}.
%
The key technologies driving this expansion include the memory optimisation technique \textit{FlashAttention} \citep{dao2022flashattention}, which reduces memory bottlenecks.
In addition, advanced position embedding techniques (e.g., \textit{RoPE}, see \cref{sec:large_context}) enable models to generalise to sequence lengths far beyond their training data.
These advances make it possible to fit the entire \citet{bb2} textbook into a context window.
%
However, capability does not imply efficiency or accuracy.
Limitations persist, even if a context window is large enough to accommodate an entire textbook, such as \citet{bb2}, comprising $115145$ tokens \citep{gpt2025tokenizer} (\textit{GPT$-4.1-mini-2025-04-14$}).
Although modern architectures have theoretically solved the problem of input size, the effective processing of this information remains a critical challenge.
\begin{quote}
\small
\textit{“Beyond a certain length, longer isn’t always better.”} – \citet{liu2024lostmiddle}
\end{quote}
Computational complexity, memory requirements, model degradation at extreme lengths~\citep{liu2024lostmiddle}, as well as availability are challenges to overcome.
The \textbf{context window} defines the boundaries of what a model can see, but does not guarantee that the model can recall everything within those boundaries unchanged and in detail.
This distinction between \textit{nominal capacity} and \textit{effective attention} is the main challenge when attempting to process knowledge sources such as \citet{bb2} textbook.
%
\subsection{Chunking}\label{sub:chunking_prel}
Despite the expanded capacity of modern \ac{lm}s, dividing texts into smaller segments remains a necessity.
%
\begin{definition}
\textbf{Chunking} is the process of dividing longer textual sequences into smaller, contextually meaningful segments (\textit{chunks}).
\end{definition}
\textit{Chunking}, for example, transforms the continuous text of a textbook (\citet{bb2}) into discrete units with predefined properties.

The \textit{chunks} produced during the \textit{Chunking} can then be further processed individually or retrieved specifically.
The two main reasons why \textit{Chunking} is necessary are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Constraint Compliance:} It ensures that the input sequences remain within the context window limits of a \ac{lm}.
    \item \textbf{Retrieval Precision:} \textit{Chunks} allow for the retrieval of specific, relevant information and knowledge.
    Irrelevant or distracting content can be effectively decreased within the resulting \ac{lm} input.
\end{enumerate}
%
\subsubsection{Chunking Strategies}\label{subsub:chunking_strat}
%
Several \textit{chunking strategies} are used to structure input sequences, thereby balancing size and semantic integrity:
%
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fixed-size chunking:}
    The text is divided into chunks of equal size based on a defined length (e.g., $512$ \textit{tokens}).
    This approach usually disrupts and thus looses the coherent contextual meaning.
    
    For example, the preprocessed text from \citet{bb2} would generate $225$ chunks if processed by \textit{fixed-size chunking}. 
    %
    \item \textbf{Sliding window:} 
    Overlapping chunks are created by shifting a fixed window across the input with some adaptable overlap (e.g., $1000$-token chunks with $4000$-token overlap, see \cref{sub:hybrid_chunking_sol}).
    In this process, each \textit{token} \enquote{attends} to a fixed-size window of \textit{tokens} around it.
    Stacking multiple layers of overlapping attention allows for a large receptive field.
    This approach helps maintain contextual continuity across boundaries~\citep{beltagy2020longformerlongdocumenttransformer}.
    Using preprocessed text from \citet{bb2} as an example, the \textit{sliding window} approach would generate $192$ chunks.
    %
    \item \textbf{Sentence- or paragraph-based chunking:} Input is segmented based on natural linguistic units.
    Semantically related sections are divided based on their syntactic structure.
    This tends to lead to inconsistent semantic integrity. 
    Chunks of different sizes are created due to varying sentence and paragraph lengths.
    Using \citet{bb2} as an example:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Approximately $2.100$ sentences within $\sim500$ paragraphs
        \item Transitions from the $3LGM^2$ graph examples to sequences
        \item Abbreviations
        \item Table of Contents
    \end{itemize}
    Depending on the syntactic analysis of certain content, such as linearised graphs and the corresponding division of the various parts, \numrange{2,000}{4,000} chunks would be generated of the book content.
    %
    \item \textbf{Semantic Chunking:}
    \textbf{Semantic Chunking} is a context-aware chunking method.
    It uses vector embeddings to identify semantic breaks within the text based on changes in topic.
    
    One such method is \textit{interquartile breakpoint detection} (IBD). 
    \textit{IBD} treats significant changes of topic as statistical outliers.
    By analysing the distribution of semantic distances between consecutive sentences, the method calculates a dynamic threshold based on the \textit{interquartile range} (IQR).
    A chunk boundary is only created if the similarity distance between the respective consecutive sentences exceeds a statistical threshold.
    This ensures that the chunks remain semantically coherent and self-contained.
    %
    \item \textbf{Hybrid Chunking:}
    This hybrid strategy combines multiple methods (e.g., \textit{semantic} and \textit{fixed-size}) while making use of their respective strengths.
    Generating multiple chunk variations for the same text increases the probability of retrieving relevant context.
    \end{itemize}
\section{Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)}\label{sec:rag_prel}
%
While the \ac{lcw} approach focusses on the \textit{technical} feasibility of processing a book in context, \ac{rag} addresses the \textit{functional} requirements of accessing specific knowledge within a book.
%

For a practitioner or student, the goal is rarely to process and access the entire content of a textbook, such as \citetitle{bb2}, at once.

Instead, a practitioner or student needs to identify precise answers to specific questions.
\ac{rag} functions as an intermediary, retrieving relevant context as needed, posing as an alternative to the usage of \ac{lcw}. 

The following sections describe the typical process and the components required for the \ac{rag} method within the context of this thesis:
\begin{center}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Retrieval} 
    \item \textit{Augmentation}
    \item \textit{Generation}
\end{enumerate}
\end{center}
% 
\subsection{Databases}\label{subsec:db}
%
Before discussing how to retrieve information and knowledge using a \ac{rag} system, we must first investigate where and how this knowledge is stored.
\begin{definition}\label{def:database}
    A \textbf{database} is a collection of related data\citep{limited2010introduction}. 
    It is used and designed to efficiently handle data storage, retrieval, and management on computer systems. 
    It contains and arranges data in a manner that makes it accessible to a variety of applications.
\end{definition}
\acp{db} are essential for the efficient storing and managing of large amounts of data. 
How the respective data is stored is affected by both the specific needs of the application and the architectural design of the database model used.
%
\subsubsection{Database Architectures}\label{sec:db_architectures}
\paragraph{Relational Databases}\label{par:rela_db}
\textbf{Relational Databases} organise data using structured tables.
Data within \textbf{Relational Databases} can be added, modified and retrieved using the \textbf{Structured Query Language} (SQL).

Relational databases can \enquote{map} relationships between data points in their structure (e.g. ~$\textit{doctor} \xrightarrow{\text{works in}} \textit{hospital}$).
However, other database types are more suitable for complex contextual relationships particularly within \textit{unstructured data}.
%
\paragraph{Unstructured data}\label{par:unstr_data}
%
\begin{definition}\label{def:unstr_data}
    \textbf{Unstructured data} refers to data that does not have a predefined data model or fixed schema.
\end{definition}
%
A \textit{data model} represents the formal logic determining how the respective data elements are structured, how they relate to one another and how they can be stored.
Whereas, a schema is the physical implementation of a data model.
If the schema is \enquote{fixed}, every record follows the exact same structure and data type.
\textbf{Unstructured data} includes formats such as text documents, images, audio, and video files, where the data is not organised in a tabular or relational structure. 
The original content, as well as the preprocessed content by \citeauthor{Paul_Keller} within \citet{bb2} represents such \textit{unstructured data}.
Therefore, it does not have a clear data model and a fixed schema and makes storage, retrieval and analyse difficult when using traditional database systems (\textit{Relational \ac{db}}).
%
\paragraph{NoSQL Databases}\label{par:nosql_db}
\textbf{NoSQL Databases} are known for their flexible schemas that do not  making them ideal for the handling of unstructured data.
This category of \ac{db} includes various architectures, such \textit{graph databases}.
%
\paragraph{Graph Databases}\label{par:graph_db}
\textbf{Graph databases} are designed to store and manage data as a collection of nodes.
A node represents an entity, while the edges connected to a node capture the relationships between these entities \citep{Robinson2015}.
The graph structure allows for an efficient processing and modelling of highly interconnected data.

Due to these features, \textit{graph databases} are particularly suitable for applications such as social networks, recommendation engines, and knowledge graphs \citep{Angles2008}.

In the context of \ac{rag}, \textit{graph databases} particularly support the retrieval of context-related information and knowledge.
They use the natural relationships embedded in the data, thereby improving the coherence and accuracy of the generated answers.
%
Using the ability  approach is at the centre of the \ac{snik} project described in \cref{sec:snik_project}, for example.
%
\subsection{Vector Databases}\label{sec:vector_db}
\begin{definition}
    A \textbf{Vector Database} is a database optimised for storing and querying vector embeddings.
\end{definition}
Vector databases operate in a continuous multi-dimensional semantic space.
Data (e.g., text chunks of \citet{bb2}) is transformed into vectors (see \cref{subsec:token_prel}, \cref{sec:encoding} and \cref{sec:embeddings}).
The number of \textbf{dimensions} corresponds to the output vector size of the embedding model.
The \textit{BAAI/bge-small-en} standard model would generate $384$ dimensions.
For comparison, \textit{OpenAi}s \texttt{text-embedding-3-small} would generate $1536$ \citep{OpenAi2024embeddings}.
Each dimension represents a latent semantic feature that allows the \ac{db} to capture contextual meaning.
%
\subsubsection{Database Management System}\label{subsub:dbm}
%
\begin{definition}\label{def:dbm}
    A \ac{dbms} is a software system that is designed to create a \ac{db} and execute operations on it.
\end{definition}
These operations are responsible for the definition, manipulation, and efficient retrieval of the stored data. 
The responsibility of a \ac{dbms} is that prior, during and after operations the databases security and potential recovery is ensured during a failure or unforeseen process sequence. \citep{limited2010introduction}
A database and \ac{dbms} together are defined as a \textit{database system}.  
%
\subsection{Retrieval Methods}\label{subsec:rag_retrieval}
%
Various retrieval methods exist to extract data from a data source, such as \ac{db}.
Depending on the database architecture and the structure and complexity of the query, different retrieval methods are used.
\textbf{Keyword search} and \textbf{vector based similarity search} are two examples of these methods.
\textit{Keyword search} represents a traditional and {vector based similarity search} an advanced modern method.
Each method is suitable for specific data and query features and requirements.
%
\subsection{Keyword Based Search}\label{subsec:keyword}
%
\textbf{Keyword-based} search is a fundamental retrieval method that retrieves data containing specific keywords. 
It is a simple and direct approach.
However, it tends to be less effective for complex queries that require a semantic understanding and coherent retrieval of the context.
%
\subsection{Vector Based Similarity Search}\label{sec:vector_simil_search}
\textbf{Vector based similarity search} retrieves relevant data by comparing vector embeddings (see \cref{sec:embeddings}).
The \textbf{Approximate Nearest  Neighbor (ANN)} algorithms used within vector databases allow for \textbf{similarity search}.
\textit{Similarity search} finds vectors that are geometrically closest (most similar) to a query vector.
The proximity is typically quantified using mathematical distance metrics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cosine Similarity:} Measures the cosine of the angle between two vectors.
    It is the standard for text embeddings, where a value of $1$ implies identical directions ($=$ identical meaning).
    \item \textbf{Euclidean Distance ($L2$):} Measures the straight-line distance between vector points.
    \item \textbf{Dot Product:} Calculates the sum of the products of corresponding vector components.  
\end{itemize}
This semantic retrieval capability is essential and highly beneficial for the \ac{rag} architecture.
It ensures that the respective \ac{lm} receives a high-quality input for the \textit{Generation} step.
%
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Retrieval} \\
    The user query is converted into a vector embedding.
    A similarity search is performed within a vector \ac{db}.
    The \textbf{top-k} (e.g., \texttt{k}$= 15$ most similar/relevant) chunks are identified.
    The variable \textbf{top-k} is introduced as the individual limit for the amount of potential chunks. 
    These chunks can be further filtered manually or by a \textbf{similarity threshold}.
    This ensures that only the most relevant vectors (chunks) are retrieved. 
    \item \textbf{Augmentation}\\
    The retrieved chunks are combined with the original query to form an enriched prompt for the \ac{lm} during \textit{Generation}.
    \item \textbf{Generation}\\
    The respective \ac{lm} processes this \textit{augmented} prompt to generate an answer based on the provided context.
    This step is referred to as \textit{in context learning}.
\end{enumerate}
%
\section{Benefits and Distinction}
The potential \textbf{benefits of \ac{rag}} include enhanced factual consistency and scope of knowledge in highly specific domains.
It can also provide answers based on current data without additional training.

However, the effectiveness and quality of a \ac{rag} system are fundamentally dependent on the performance of its components.
As an alternative to resource-intensive continual pre-training, \ac{rag} offers a more flexible and efficient approach to knowledge augmentation. \citet{Paul_Keller} highlighted \ac{rag} as a promising direction for future work.
%
\section{Interacting with Language Models: Inference}\label{sec:inference}
%
As explained in \cref{def:inference}, an \ac{lm} applies its inherent knowledge learned in the \textit{inference phase} to perform tasks based on new inputs, referred to as \textbf{prompts}.
%
\subsection{Prompting Techniques}
%
The formulation of the prompt guides the \ac{llm} response generation.
Key strategies for prompting:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Zero-shot Prompting:}\label{item:zero_shot} 
    The \ac{lm} receives only the direct query with the included instruction and demands without any additional illustrative examples.
    Therefore, success relies only on the inherent understanding and instruction-following capabilities of the \ac{lm} which is developed during \textit{pre-training}.
    \citet{Paul_Keller} applied the \textit{zero-shot} method to assess the domain-specific knowledge gained by the \textit{Llama 2} model through continual pre-training.
    %
    \item \textbf{Few-shot Prompting:}\label{item:few_shot} 
    The prompt is augmented with a certain number of input-output examples.
    These examples are intended to show how the task was previously solved or how it can be solved in the same or different scenario.
    In this way, an exemplary solution path or pattern of reasoning is demonstrated.
    This textual learning can guide the model to more accurate or appropriate responses.
    \item \textbf{Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting:}\label{item:cot} This more advanced technique requires the model to articulate its thought process step by step in response to the task at hand before providing the final answer.
    This is often done by including examples of such reasoning in the prompt or by explicitly instructing the model to \enquote{think step by step}.
    \ac{cot} can improve performance on tasks that require multi-step logic.
\end{enumerate}
Effective prompting can sometimes involve adapting the input format to the specific model's characteristics. 
For example, \citet{Paul_Keller} found it necessary to rephrase direct questions into sentence completions for the base \textit{Llama 2} model.
%
\subsection{Generation Parameters}
%
The process of selecting subsequent \textit{tokens} during text generation is typically probabilistic (see \cref{def:inference} and can be modulated using several parameters:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Temperature:}\label{item:temperature} This parameter scales the probabilities of the next potential \textit{tokens}.
    \textit{Lower temperatures}, approaching a \textit{temp} value of $0$ make the selection process more deterministic.
    The most probable \textit{tokens} are favoured, thereby leading to more focused, yet potentially more repetitive outputs.
    \textit{Higher temperatures} increase randomness.
    Less likely \textit{tokens} may be selected, which can improve diversity and creativity but the output might suffer from an increased incoherence.
    \citet{Paul_Keller} chose a temperature of $0.9$ for the evaluations, indicating that some level of variability was accepted.
    The \textit{temperature} setting have a direct influence on the reproducibility.
    If multiple answes are generated fo the same query, the \textit{temperature} determines the degree of determinism and resulting comparability.
    \item \textbf{Top-k Sampling:}\label{item:top-k} The choice for the next \textit{token} to be predicted is limited to the top \textit{k} options.
    \item \textbf{Top-p (Nucleus) Sampling:}\label{item:top-p} The next generated \textit{token} is selected from the smallest possible set of \textit{tokens} whose cumulative probability value exceeds a threshold \textit{p}.
\end{enumerate}
To ensure reproducibility of the evaluation results, accurate documentation and adjustment of these parameters are important.
%
\subsection{Access and Deployment of Language Models}
%
The method of accessing a \ac{lm} for inference influences capability, accessibility and control in a potential application.
%
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{API-based Inference:} 
    Through \ac{api}s, \ac{lm} that are provided and hosted by third-party providers can be accessed for individual application.
    This allows the use of high-performance \acp{llm} without potential local hardware restrictions.
    The \ac{api} usage usually comes with usage costs, privacy considerations, and limited control over the execution environment.
\item \textbf{Local Inference:}
    Execution of open-source models (such as the \textit{Llama-2} variants trained and evaluated by \citet{Paul_Keller}) on user-controlled hardware.
    The hardware used can range from single-user computers to institutional computing clusters (such as those used by \citeauthor{Paul_Keller} at the University of Leipzig). 
    This approach offers better control, improves data security and avoids direct costs per use.
    At the same time, it is fundamentally associated with indirect costs and resources, such as available computing power (RAM and GPU memory) and is thus often limited.
    Techniques such as \textit{model quantisation}, which reduces the numerical precision of model parameters (e.g., to 8-bit or 4-bit integers), can significantly reduce memory and computational requirements for local inference.
    \citet{Paul_Keller} pointed to \textit{$8$-bit} quantisation for inference tasks in certain hardware configurations.
\end{itemize}

\section{The SNIK-project}\label{sec:snik_project}
\subsection{The SNIK-Ontology}
%
The \ac{snik} ontology is a domain ontology developed at the Institute of Medical Informatics, Statistics and Epidemiology (IMISE) at the University of Leipzig.
An expert interview dealing with \ac{him}, text books on \ac{him} and frameworks were converted to a domain ontology.
The knowledge of these entities was concentrated and modeled as a \ac{rdf}, while each entity is represented as a sub-ontology within \ac{snik}.
The sub-ontologies are theoretically independent.
%
\subsection{The SNIK-Graph}

\subsection{The SNIK-Quiz}
%
\citet{snikquiz} was developed to further simplify and diversify access to the knowledge contained in \ac{snik}.
\enquote{[It] is designed as a multiple choice quiz with at most four possible answers and published as an open source web application [\dots]} \citep{snikquiz}.
Users can interact with the application without a background in semantic web technologies and use it with various end devices.

Direct feedback on the complexity of the questions and their respective category, while using the application, allows for a personalised experience. 
The results show success as \ac{him} knowledge within \ac{snik} can be extracted by an application.
Using that appliaction the knowledge can be processed and used to then semi-automatically generating individualised questions.

Although preferentially basic questions referring to singular facts were mainly positively evaluated by users. 
The qualitative feedback on complex questions is mixed.\citep{snikquiz}
Further integration into courses and evaluation will show the possibilities of the application and possibly highlight its limitations.

It thereby also provides understanding of the basic relationships and structures in the domain-specific area of \ac{him}.
