Extracting fragmented knowledge from academic textbooks is both complex and time-consuming. In medical informatics, intuitive and efficient access to domain-specific knowledge is of great educational importance. In this study, a reproducible, containerized open-source application was developed to make the knowledge within the standard textbook *Health Information Systems: Architectures and Strategies* by Winter et al. (2023) automatically accessible. The application implements and compares two question-answering pipelines: Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and Large Context Windows (LCW). The system was evaluated using an asynchronous "LLM-as-a-judge" framework powered by Gemini-2.5-Flash to answer exam questions from the textbook as well as from Master's level medical informatics modules at the University of Leipzig. Rigorous benchmarks across high-performing language models showed that RAG is the slightly superior and more stable method. Operating at a fraction of the cost, RAG sets critical factual boundaries that prevent hallucination and secure high semantic robustness against spelling mistakes. In contrast, the LCW method remains highly vulnerable to input noise. Under strict evaluations, GPT-4.1-Mini achieved the highest RAG F1 score of 0.518 on textbook queries, while the free open-source alternative Qwen3-VL-32B achieved an LCW F1 score of 0.511. Under a more lenient credit-based exam policy, GPT-4.1-Mini answered up to 89% of exam questions correctly or partially correctly. These results demonstrate the feasibility of automatically solving medical informatics exams using un-tuned, pre-trained language models, and establish a reliable, reproducible evaluation framework for comparing modern knowledge retrieval systems.
